\begin{abstract}
Machine-learning studies of crop yield usually optimize average predictive accuracy, but
agricultural risk management also needs event-level explanations: why did a particular
crop-state-year fall below its expected yield? We present Sparse Counterfactual Anomaly
Attribution (SCAA), a model-based framework that detects detrended low-yield anomalies
and explains them using sparse, physically grouped extreme-weather edits. Daily NASA
POWER weather and USDA yield records are aggregated for four crops, twelve U.S. states,
and 1990--2025. A forward-time ExtraTrees yield model attains $R^2=0.809$ for
2016--2021 and $R^2=0.808$ for 2019--2025. After crop-state detrending, 214 of 1,257
observations are flagged as low-yield anomalies. The main submission protocol is
retrospective leave-one-event-year-out grouped SCAA: for every anomaly year, the
residual model is fitted after excluding all rows from that year. Under this stricter
protocol, the median recoverable fraction is 0.116 and 5.6\% of anomalies (12 of 214)
exceed a recovery threshold of $\phi \geq 0.5$. Residual-model validation is modest for
all held-out event-year rows ($R^2=0.085$) and weak on anomaly rows ($R^2=-5.152$), so
SCAA is not framed as a high-skill residual forecasting system. Event-year consistency
is reported as a limited sanity check rather than primary validation because broad
heat/drought labels are also matched by simple null baselines. The contribution is a
reproducible, conservative workflow for retrospective model-based weather-driver
attribution on a detrended yield-residual scale, not a new yield prediction model.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} crop yield; extreme weather; counterfactual explanation; yield anomaly; interpretable machine learning; drought; heat stress.

\section{Introduction}
Extreme weather can produce large agricultural losses, with drought, heat, excess
water, and cold events affecting crop production at regional and global scales
\citep{Lesk2016,Zampieri2017,Vogel2019}. Standard crop-yield machine-learning
workflows demonstrate that weather and climate variables can support accurate yield
forecasting \citep{Paudel2021,Meroni2021,Khaki2019,LengHall2020}, but prediction alone
does not answer the event-level question needed for risk interpretation: which weather
driver does the model identify as the strongest explanation for a specific low-yield
crop-state-year?

This paper reframes yield interpretation as anomaly attribution. We first separate
long-run crop-state yield trend from short-run variation, following detrended yield
residual analysis \citep{Ray2015,Lu2017,Meng2024}. We then detect low-yield anomalies
and ask a counterfactual question on the raw residual scale: what sparse, feasible
change to a weather-driver group would move the predicted detrended residual toward a
normal season? Unlike feature-importance methods such as SHAP or LIME
\citep{LundbergLee2017,Ribeiro2016}, this yields an event-level recovery quantity in
t ha$^{-1}$ and a crop-specific claim.

The contribution is not a new yield predictor. The contribution is a reproducible
workflow that combines detrended yield anomalies, retrospective leave-one-event-year-out
residual modelling, physically grouped extreme-weather drivers, and sparse
counterfactual recovery. Figure \ref{fig:workflow} summarizes the pipeline.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{Crop-Yield Prediction and Weather Features}
Large-scale crop-yield forecasting has been studied with random forests, neural
networks, and other statistical or machine-learning approaches
\citep{Paudel2021,Khaki2019,Shahhosseini2021}. In-season forecasting under small-data
conditions is also active, especially for grain systems \citep{Meroni2021}. These
studies support the predictive modeling layer of our work, but they mainly optimize
forecasting skill rather than event-level explanation.

\subsection{Extreme-Weather Yield Anomalies}
Prior work links yield anomalies to climate extremes, including heatwaves, drought,
water excess, hot-dry compound events, and crop-specific weather relationships
\citep{Zampieri2017,Vogel2019,Heino2023,Sjulgard2023}. Stage-specific weather effects
are also important because temperature, rainfall, frost, and radiation can matter
differently during crop development \citep{Schierhorn2021}. These studies motivate our
driver groups: heat, drought/dry-spell, frost/cold, excess rainfall, and radiation.

\subsection{Counterfactual and Event-Level Attribution}
Counterfactual explanations ask for minimal changes that would alter a model outcome
\citep{Wachter2018}. Diverse and feasible counterfactual methods emphasize proximity,
sparsity, plausibility, and actionable constraints
\citep{Mothilal2020,Ustun2019,Poyiadzi2020,Verma2024}. Climate-event attribution
literature also emphasizes careful event definition and cautious interpretation
\citep{Hannart2016,Otto2017,Oldenborgh2021}. We use the counterfactual form for
model-based explanation of yield residuals and report consistency with known stress
groups rather than formal event-attribution effects.

\section{Data}
The empirical unit is a crop-state-year. The processed frame contains 1,257
observations for Barley, Canola, Oats, and Wheat across twelve U.S. states from
1990--2025. Yields are in t ha$^{-1}$ and are derived from USDA NASS yield records
\citep{USDANASSQuickStats}. Weather features are derived from NASA POWER daily records
\citep{NASAPOWER2025} and include rainfall totals, dry spells, heavy-rain events, heat
days, heatwaves, growing-degree days, frost/cold metrics, and radiation summaries.

The weather extraction uses one representative latitude-longitude coordinate per state.
Thus, all crops in a state share the same daily weather series before crop-specific
growing-season windows are applied. The design is transparent and reproducible, but it
should be interpreted as representative-coordinate state weather rather than a
crop-area-weighted state average or a within-state grid average. Growing season windows
are crop- and state-aware, with spring and winter windows represented in the processed
frame.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Yield Skill Baseline}
We retain a forward-time yield model to verify that the weather and crop-state features
contain predictive signal. Numeric variables are median-imputed and standardized;
categorical crop and state identifiers are one-hot encoded. An ExtraTrees regressor is
trained under two forward-time splits: train through 2015 and test 2016--2021; then
train through 2018 and test 2019--2025.

\subsection{Detrending and Anomaly Screening}
For each crop-state series, a linear trend is fit to yield:
\begin{equation}
  y_{c,s,t} = \alpha_{c,s} + \beta_{c,s} t + \epsilon_{c,s,t}.
\end{equation}
The raw detrended residual is
\begin{equation}
  r_i = y_i - y^{trend}_i,
\end{equation}
with unit t ha$^{-1}$. The standardized residual $z_i$ is used only to screen
low-yield anomalies. An observation is flagged when $z_i < -1.0$. We use the term
``low-yield anomaly'' rather than ``extreme anomaly'' because this threshold is meant
to capture a broad set of below-trend events for attribution.

\subsection{Retrospective Leave-One-Event-Year-Out Grouped SCAA}
The main submission method is retrospective leave-one-event-year-out grouped SCAA. For
each anomaly year $t^\star$, we train a residual model $f_{-t^\star}$ on all
crop-state-year rows except those from $t^\star$. This prevents the model from directly
learning the same event year being explained. It is not a fully prospective forecast,
however, because observations after $t^\star$ may remain in the training set. The
target is the raw detrended yield residual \texttt{trend\_residual\_t\_ha}, not the
standardized residual. The fitted model maps weather features, crop, and state to
predicted residual $\hat r_i$ on the same t ha$^{-1}$ scale.

For an anomaly $i$, grouped SCAA evaluates driver groups one at a time. For a group
$g$, all weather features in that group are moved toward their observed median for the
same region-window support, giving a diagnostic edited feature vector
$\bm{x}^{cf(g)}_i$. The observed detrended yield shortfall is
\begin{equation}
  \Delta_i = \max(0, y^{trend}_i - y_i).
\end{equation}
The recovered yield gap for the best group edit is
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{recovered\_gap}_{i} =
  \min\left(\max(0, \hat r^{cf}_i - \hat r_i), \Delta_i\right),
\end{equation}
and the recoverable fraction is
\begin{equation}
  \phi_i =
  \begin{cases}
    \mathrm{recovered\_gap}_{i}/\Delta_i, & \Delta_i > 0,\\
    0, & \Delta_i = 0.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
A crop-state-year is labelled weather-driven when $\phi_i \geq 0.5$.

The grouped edit is a diagnostic sparse edit designed to isolate one physical driver
group. It is not assumed to be a complete season that occurred historically. For that
reason, we also report observed-analog counterfactuals: real non-anomalous seasons
from the same crop-state, or the same region-window fallback, used as a plausibility
check for weather-vector replacement.

\subsection{Residual-Model Validation}
Because grouped SCAA operates on the raw detrended residual scale, we separately
evaluate the residual model used for attribution. We report $R^2$, RMSE, and MAE for
\texttt{trend\_residual\_t\_ha} under a forward-time residual split and under the same
retrospective leave-one-event-year-out protocol used by grouped SCAA. These metrics do
not replace the forward-time yield model; they verify how much residual-scale signal is
available before diagnostic counterfactual recovery is interpreted.

\subsection{Robustness and Extensions}
We include four robustness or extension layers. First, in-sample grouped SCAA is kept
as an exploratory comparison to show the effect of removing year holdout. Second,
observed-analog counterfactuals test whether recovery is possible using historically
observed normal seasons. Third, anomaly-threshold and detrending sensitivity tables
check whether the anomaly set is an artifact of a single screening choice. Fourth,
crop-specific vulnerability profiles stress-test each crop only on observed
crop-state-year combinations. An early-warning extension trains anomaly classifiers
from early and early-plus-mid season weather features, with calibration-derived
uncertainty bands \citep{Anderson2024,Singh2024,Farag2025}.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Yield Skill and Anomalies}
The yield model reproduces strong forward-time predictive skill
(Table~\ref{tab:model_performance}; Figure~\ref{fig:yield_prediction}). The 2016--2021
split attains $R^2=0.809$ and RMSE 0.525 t ha$^{-1}$; the 2019--2025 split attains
$R^2=0.808$ and RMSE 0.536 t ha$^{-1}$. After detrending, 214 of 1,257 observations are
flagged as low-yield anomalies. The anomaly timeline highlights 2012, 2021, and 2022
as event years used for the consistency check (Figure~\ref{fig:anomaly_timeline}).

\subsection{Residual-Model Validation}
Table~\ref{tab:residual_model_validation} reports the predictive skill of the
residual model used by grouped SCAA. The forward-time residual split has $R^2=0.093$,
RMSE 0.431 t ha$^{-1}$, and MAE 0.326 t ha$^{-1}$. Under retrospective
leave-one-event-year-out evaluation across all held-out rows, $R^2=0.085$, RMSE is
0.369 t ha$^{-1}$, and MAE is 0.284 t ha$^{-1}$. On the anomaly rows alone, $R^2$ is
negative ($R^2=-5.152$), indicating that anomaly-level residual magnitudes are
difficult to predict directly. We therefore interpret SCAA as a diagnostic recovery
framework with bounded counterfactual edits, not as a high-skill residual forecasting
model.

\subsection{Method Comparison}
Table~\ref{tab:method_scorecard} and Figure~\ref{fig:raw_metrics} compare methods by
raw metrics only. The retrospective leave-one-event-year-out method is more
conservative than the in-sample grouped method: median recoverable fraction decreases
from 0.589 to 0.116, and the weather-driven rate decreases from 73.4\% to 5.6\% (12
of 214 anomalies). This reduction is expected because each event year is excluded
before attribution. We therefore use leave-one-event-year-out grouped SCAA for the
main submission claims, while the in-sample grouped result is treated as exploratory.
Observed-analog counterfactuals show larger recovery because they replace the full
weather vector with a real normal season; they are used as a plausibility comparison
rather than the main sparse explanation.

\subsection{Event-Year Consistency Check}
We pre-specified broad expected stress groups for 2012, 2021, and 2022 using external
NOAA/NCEI, National Weather Service, and Drought.gov/NIDIS evidence
\citep{NOAA2012Drought,NWS2012Drought,DroughtGov2021NorthernPlains,NOAA2022Drought,NOAA2022AugustDrought}.
This check asks whether the SCAA dominant groups are consistent with those external
stress categories; it does not validate formal event drivers. Retrospective
leave-one-event-year-out grouped SCAA matches the pre-specified expected groups in
91.4\% of event-year rows: 100\% in 2012, 100\% in 2021, and 76.9\% in 2022.

The null baselines in Table~\ref{tab:event_null_baselines} show why this result should
be treated cautiously. Always assigning drought, always assigning heat, or assigning
the most frequent event-year SCAA driver also matches 100\% of the broad expected
stress groups. The event-year consistency check is therefore retained as a limited
sanity check that the model outputs do not contradict the pre-specified stress
categories, not as evidence that SCAA outperforms trivial event labels.

The vulnerability profile answers which weather driver most reduces which crop in the

with median effect $-0.103$ t ha$^{-1}$; and Canola is most sensitive to heat exposure,

\section{Discussion}
The revised results support a conservative paper framing. The retrospective
leave-one-event-year-out SCAA protocol produces lower recovery than the in-sample
grouped method, but it better addresses external-by-year attribution concerns because
the event year is absent from the residual-model fit. Its strongest contribution is
therefore not high recovery for every anomaly; it is a transparent event-level protocol
that reports crop, state, year, driver group, dominant feature, recovered t ha$^{-1}$,
and the residual-scale recovery formula.

The residual model itself is weak on anomaly rows ($R^2=-5.152$), so high-recovery
cases should be read as conservative diagnostic explanations within the fitted model
rather than as evidence of broad residual-forecasting skill.

The threshold sensitivity analysis shows that the anomaly count changes from 214 at
$z<-1.0$ to 85 at $z<-1.5$ and 30 at $z<-2.0$, while the leave-one-event-year-out
median recoverable fraction remains close to 0.12 across thresholds. Detrending
robustness is moderate: quadratic detrending has Jaccard overlap 0.774 with the linear
anomaly set, while a centered seven-year rolling baseline has overlap 0.550. We keep
linear detrending because it is transparent and low-parameter, but report these
alternatives to make the screening choice explicit.

There are several limitations. The counterfactuals are model-internal predictive
associations and should not be read as formal climate-event attribution. State-level
representative-coordinate weather hides within-state variation in weather, irrigation,
soil, cultivar, and management. The detrending step is also estimated over the full
1990--2025 retrospective crop-state series; an operational early-warning deployment
would require rolling or past-only trend estimation before anomaly screening. The
grouped edit changes several related features toward medians and may not represent a
physically complete weather season; observed-analog counterfactuals partly address this
by using historical seasons that actually occurred. Finally, the early-warning extension
uses realized early-season weather, not operational seasonal forecasts.

\section{Conclusion}
SCAA detects detrended low-yield anomalies and explains them through sparse,
physically grouped weather counterfactuals on a raw residual scale. On U.S. Barley,
Canola, Oats, and Wheat across 1990--2025, the retrospective leave-one-event-year-out
grouped-SCAA protocol produces conservative crop-state-year claims such as
drought/dry-spell stress in North Dakota Canola 2012, with explicit recovered
t ha$^{-1}$ and recoverable fractions. The result is a reproducible bridge between
crop-yield prediction, interpretable machine learning, and cautious diagnostic
event-level weather-driver analysis.
